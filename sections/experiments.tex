\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Evaluation Protocol}

We evaluate 39 RGB-D loop-closure cases from 18 Kimera-Multi
sequences~\cite{tian23arxiv_kimeramultiexperiments} and 12 LiDAR cases from
six KITTI sequences~\cite{Geiger2013IJRR}.  Every method reconstructs the
same temporal interval in a case and is evaluated against the same
ground-truth (GT) point cloud.  For Kimera-Multi, GT is restricted to that
interval and to a 5 m tube around the official GT trajectory.  For KITTI,
GT-registered LiDAR points are retained within 25 m of the evaluated GT
trajectory segment, matching the mapper's maximum ray length.  Predictions
not already expressed in the GT frame are aligned by a RANSAC--Umeyama
$\mathrm{Sim}(3)$ fitted to timestamp-matched positions from their generating
and GT trajectories; no mesh-to-GT registration is performed.  Evaluation
replays frozen inputs offline.  The maps are nevertheless updated
sequentially: after each correction, the returned TSDF replaces the current
Voxblox volume and fusion continues to the next loop-closure checkpoint.

\noindent\textbf{Baselines.}
Uncorrected TSDF integrates the measurements using the pre-correction
trajectory.  Open3D and Wavemap are corrected-pose full-replay baselines:
Open3D reconstructs an independent TSDF~\cite{zhou2018open3dmodernlibrary3d},
whereas Wavemap rebuilds a hierarchical wavelet occupancy map
~\cite{reijgwart2023wavemap}.  Voxgraph instead builds rigid TSDF submaps
online and optimizes their poses after loop closure~\cite{reijgwart2020voxgraph}.
Voxblox reintegration is the representation-matched reference, rebuilding the
same TSDF from all raw measurements using the corrected
poses~\cite{Oleynikova_2017}.  WarpVox corrects the uncorrected TSDF from
retained sensor-frame support and the pre/post-correction trajectories.  In the
offline benchmark, Stage 1 is reproduced by replaying only the recorded
keyframes after the pre-correction mesh is available; this replay is used only
for deterministic evaluation.  In online operation, the same associations are
accumulated as keyframes arrive, so loop-time correction does not replay
historical frames.

For prediction $P$, reference $G$, and distance threshold $r$, precision and
recall are computed by bidirectional nearest-neighbour tests, with
$F_r=2P_rR_r/(P_r+R_r)$.  We report F@10, F@25, and F@50 in percent at
$r=0.10$, $0.25$, and $0.50$ m, respectively.  All methods share the temporal
crop, spatial region, two-million-point sample, random seed, and thresholds of
their case.  TSDF methods use a 0.25 m voxel size and a 1.0 m truncation
distance in the main experiment; Wavemap uses a matched 0.25 m finest
resolution.  CPU times are measured on an AMD Ryzen 7 9700X with 64 GB RAM.

\subsection{Event-Triggered Comparison with Kimera-PGMO}

Kimera-PGMO and WarpVox occupy different points in the map-correction
pipeline. Kimera-PGMO continuously maintains a MeshFrontend deformation
graph, whereas WarpVox is applied when a global trajectory revision becomes
available. We therefore compare their final states on ten complete
Kimera-Multi bags. Both methods start from the same 5 cm Voxblox pre-mesh and
use the same final VIO-to-PGMO trajectory correction. Kimera-PGMO additionally
exploits the MeshFrontend's timestamped graph associations (3 m graph spacing,
three neighbours, and a 20 s horizon), while WarpVox independently constructs
support from recorded keyframes and VIO poses.

For bags exhibiting variation across asynchronous replays, we use the
complete trajectory--mesh pair with the lowest ATE, selected independently of
mesh accuracy, and apply its trajectory correction to both methods.
Kimera-PGMO outputs a corrected mesh; WarpVox instead reconstructs a 5 cm
TSDF. Since the native pre-mesh is not accompanied by its source TSDF volume,
we use the mesh-only Stage 4 in this comparison. This comparison therefore
evaluates surface correction rather than source-support transfer.

A single WarpVox update improves the mean F@25/F@50 by 1.24/3.03 pp while
reducing cumulative core time by $9.27\times$. It achieves higher F@50 on
eight of ten bags, with per-bag differences ranging from $-0.44$ to
$+11.74$ pp. These results place WarpVox at a complementary operating point:
it closely tracks, and on average exceeds, the final geometric accuracy of
continuous mesh optimization while returning a mesh-derived Voxblox TSDF
volume at substantially lower cumulative cost. Kimera-PGMO, in turn, retains
the distinct benefits of intermediate corrections and frontend temporal
associations.

\subsection{Extension to Gaussian Primitives}

Surface-target computation is not specific to a voxel grid. We apply the same
targets to a SplaTAM~\cite{keetha2024splatam} map while changing
only Gaussian centres; colour, opacity, scale, covariance, densification, and
rendering losses remain fixed.  On one sequence, the pre map obtains
17.23/35.25 F@25/F@50 and 173.93 cm depth L1.  Updating only the centres raises
these values to 23.12/40.59 and reduces depth L1 to 142.54 cm in approximately
21 s; a complete corrected-pose rebuild obtains 23.25/41.01 and 141.37 cm in
approximately 2 h.  The update also improves PSNR from 11.96 to 12.59,
MS-SSIM from 0.512 to 0.526, and LPIPS from 0.430 to 0.411.

The experiment isolates the representation-independent target mechanism from
the TSDF-specific reconstruction stage.  Gaussian splats are optimized for
differentiable surface rendering~\cite{kerbl2023gaussians}; they do not natively
encode the signed-distance and observed-free-space structure used for
obstacle-distance queries.  WarpVox therefore retains TSDF/ESDF mapping as its
primary robotics setting while extending its geometric correction to explicit
Gaussian primitives.
