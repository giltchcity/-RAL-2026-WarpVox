\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Evaluation Protocol}

We evaluate 39 RGB-D loop-closure cases from 18 Kimera-Multi
sequences~\cite{tian23arxiv_kimeramultiexperiments} and 12 LiDAR cases from
six KITTI sequences~\cite{Geiger2013IJRR}.  Every method reconstructs the
same temporal interval in a case and is evaluated against the same
ground-truth (GT) point cloud.  For Kimera-Multi, GT is restricted to that
interval and to a 5 m tube around the official GT trajectory.  For KITTI,
GT-registered LiDAR points are retained within 25 m of the evaluated GT
trajectory segment, matching the mapper's maximum ray length.  Predictions
not already expressed in the GT frame are aligned by a RANSAC--Umeyama
$\mathrm{Sim}(3)$ fitted to timestamp-matched positions from their generating
and GT trajectories; no mesh-to-GT registration is performed.

\noindent\textbf{Baselines.}
Uncorrected TSDF integrates the measurements using the pre-correction
trajectory. Open3D independently integrates them using the corrected
poses~\cite{zhou2018open3dmodernlibrary3d}. Voxgraph builds rigid TSDF submaps
online and optimizes their poses after loop closure~\cite{reijgwart2020voxgraph}.
Wavemap integrates the corrected-pose measurements into a hierarchical wavelet
occupancy map~\cite{reijgwart2023wavemap}. Full reintegration rebuilds the
Voxblox TSDF from all raw measurements using the corrected poses~\cite{Oleynikova_2017}.
WarpVox instead corrects the uncorrected TSDF from retained sensor-frame
support and the pre/post-correction trajectories, without reprocessing the raw
measurements.

For prediction $P$, reference $G$, and distance threshold $r$, precision and
recall are computed by bidirectional nearest-neighbour tests, with
$F_r=2P_rR_r/(P_r+R_r)$.  All methods share the temporal crop, spatial region,
two-million-point sample, random seed, and thresholds of their case.  All main
experiments use a 0.25 m voxel size and a 1.0 m truncation distance.  CPU times
are measured on an AMD Ryzen 7 9700X with 64 GB RAM.

\subsection{Additional Kimera-PGMO Comparison}

We report Kimera-PGMO separately from the 51-checkpoint benchmark in
Table~\ref{tab:broad_main}. Native post-PGMO ATE varied across repeated runs;
for each of four bags, we conservatively retain the lowest-ATE complete run.
Kimera$_{\mathrm{Pre}}$ is its native mesh fused along the VIO trajectory,
and Kimera-PGMO is the native post-correction mesh. WarpVox receives the same
pre-mesh and VIO-to-PGMO pose update, so this experiment compares how the two
methods propagate a common trajectory correction to an existing mesh rather
than comparing front ends. Because PGMO optimizes continuously, we sample the
three ORB-SLAM3 loop times in 1207 and the four full-sequence endpoints;
ORB-SLAM3 supplies timestamps only, yielding seven checkpoints.

On this subset, WarpVox reaches 29.59/49.15\% at F@25/F@50, compared with
25.39/43.32\% for Kimera-PGMO, gains of 4.20/5.83 pp. Across the same seven
scopes, WarpVox is $9.05\times$ faster on average and up to $11.31\times$ faster. Core time
includes Ray, Target/Ceres, ARAP, and Mesh-to-TSDF for WarpVox and graph/mesh
optimization for Kimera-PGMO; load/save, I/O, and wall-time overhead are excluded.

The only exceptions are 1207 L1, where the optimized trajectory changes
negligibly, and 1207 L3, where substantial residual error leaves little
reliable correction to propagate.

\subsection{Extension to Gaussian Primitives}

Surface-target computation is not specific to a voxel grid. We apply the same
targets to a SplaTAM~\cite{keetha2024splatam} map while changing
only Gaussian centres; colour, opacity, scale, covariance, densification, and
rendering losses remain fixed.  On one sequence, the pre map obtains
17.23/35.25 F@25/F@50 and 173.93 cm depth L1.  Updating only the centres raises
these values to 23.12/40.59 and reduces depth L1 to 142.54 cm in approximately
21 s; a complete corrected-pose rebuild obtains 23.25/41.01 and 141.37 cm in
approximately 2 h.  The update also improves PSNR from 11.96 to 12.59,
MS-SSIM from 0.512 to 0.526, and LPIPS from 0.430 to 0.411.

The experiment isolates the representation-independent target mechanism from
the TSDF-specific reconstruction stage.  Gaussian splats are optimized for
differentiable surface rendering~\cite{kerbl2023gaussians}; they do not natively
encode the signed-distance and observed-free-space structure used for
obstacle-distance queries.  WarpVox therefore retains TSDF/ESDF mapping as its
primary robotics setting while extending its geometric correction to explicit
Gaussian primitives.
